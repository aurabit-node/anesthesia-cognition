Introduction

Modern anesthesia has transformed surgery into a safe and humane discipline. However, its interaction with the brain remains complex, particularly in elderly and vulnerable populations. Cognitive outcomes now represent a critical quality metric in perioperative medicine.

Neurobiological Mechanisms

Inflammation and Microglia

Surgical trauma induces systemic inflammation, enabling cytokine penetration into the central nervous system and activation of microglial pathways that impair synaptic plasticity.

Neurotransmitter Disruption

Cholinergic deficiency, GABAergic imbalance, and glutamate excitotoxicity contribute to attention deficits and executive dysfunction.

Postoperative Delirium

Delirium is an acute fluctuating syndrome affecting up to half of elderly surgical patients and is independently associated with increased mortality, institutionalization, and long-term cognitive decline.

Postoperative Cognitive Dysfunction

POCD presents as persistent impairment in memory and executive function, often detected only by formal neuropsychological testing. While frequently reversible, it may accelerate neurodegenerative trajectories in susceptible individuals.

Prevention and Neuroprotection

  • Preoperative cognitive screening
  • Depth-of-anesthesia monitoring
  • Hemodynamic stability
  • Multimodal opioid-sparing analgesia
  • Delirium prevention bundles

Frequently Asked Questions

Does anesthesia cause permanent memory loss?

In most patients, cognitive changes are temporary. Persistent deficits occur primarily in elderly or high-risk individuals with preexisting vulnerability.

Is regional anesthesia safer for the brain?

Regional techniques may reduce cognitive risk in selected patients by limiting systemic drug exposure, though outcomes depend on multiple factors.
